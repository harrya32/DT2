**(A) Prior knowledge and coverage of the candidate policy set $\Pi$ (Q1 & Q9)**

*Is a finite, known $\Pi$ realistic?* Yes, there are many real-world applications where the set of candidate policies is finite and known *a priori*. For example, in medical settings (like our cancer case study), treatment options follow highly standardized, discrete clinical guidelines (e.g., specific chemotherapy dosages or radiotherapy schedules). Nevertheless, we do not claim that only the policies seen during training will be well-ranked by $\text{DT}^2$ .

*Do certain $\Pi$ lead to overfiting, and how do we quantify coverage?* We have found that $\text{DT}^2$ learns generalized, decision-relevant dynamics rather than merely overfitting. This is shown in $\S 6.3$, and we have now run additional experiments testing more out-of-distribution policies in continuous control environments (see our response to point (D) of Reviewer `oAwR'). Here, we see again that $\text{DT}^2$ maintains better ranking performance over standard NLL models. 

We agree that formalizing the "coverage" or distributional overlap between training and testing policies is a relevant question, which we have noted in our Limitations section as a prime direction for future work. It would be interesting to see if we can limit the policies that need to be seen during training, for efficiency purposes, but also, it would be nice to provide some guidance on what kind of unseen policies we expect to be well-ranked. However, given the complexity of this, we leave it for future work.

**(D) $\lambda$ ablation (Q6)**

When we remove the simulation loss entirely ($\lambda=1$), we find that training becomes quite unstable, and the ranking ability of the DT does not further increase, while simulation loss grows rapidly. Pure "learning-to-rank" objectives are notoriously difficult to optimize from scratch, especially as we backpropagate through recurrent rollouts to do so. The simulation loss can therefore be seen as a smoothing factor for the decision-loss landscape, making optimisation stable. We therefore do not recommend large $\lambda$ values, as we say in $\S 6.4$.

In the environments tested in this paper, FQE generally had lower regret than the DT models, and we did not generally see $\text{DT}^2$ perfectly replicate the FQE rankings.
